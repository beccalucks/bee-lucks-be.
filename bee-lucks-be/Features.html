Snowdonia: My Welsh Roots are Showing.

By Becca Thomas Loux

During August of two years ago, I learned the story of my Welsh ancestors: they fled from persecution over marrying outside of their social rank in the early 1900's.

Last August I moved to Cardiff, visited the graves of possible past relations in central Wales with my mom and tried to pick up a bit of Welsh on the side whilst completing my masters and learning Portuguese.

Somehow, in connecting with my Welsh roots, everything and nothing has changed.

Since moving to south Wales, I have spent a lot of time learning about ancient Welsh mythology, along with exploring my own connection to spirituality and symbology thoroughout my life.

For example, I've always felt a connection with the study of geology, and recently a clear piece of celestite that was a gift from a close friend has become more meaningful to me in reading about the physical symbols of Wales' past.

In so doing, I have also come across a wealth of knowlege about the Welsh, British, Scotish and Irish countryside. I've explored the rocks of _____ and hiked Hadrian's Wall. I've looked back home out off the Cliffs of Moher and thought about my destiny. I've enjoyed travel all my life, and I've always said that I felt like the whole planet was my home.

For a week in August, the van from Enterprise was our home. My partner Sochi drove me 1095 miles (according to Google Maps) from park to park, hostel to camping spot, to get a feel for the UK's National Park System and collect data for this project. Naturally, we saw some stunning views and took some amazing hikes and experienced a travel mishap or two along the way.

The view of my garden from my kitchen window here in Cardiff. Featuring: Tomas the Gecko.

Yet somehow, in exploring the UK, the train or car ride back to Cardiff was always a feeling of coming home. Don't get me wrong--this is not the first time this has happened. I've felt at home exactly six places in my life before this: Davis, California. Santa Cruz & Oakland, California. Cahuita, Costa Rica. Granada, EspaNa. Santa Barbara, California. The Lake District has Developed its Own Currency

By Becca Thomas Loux

This is a screenshot I took of the website of the Lakes District Pound. The notes themselves cannot be photographed legally but have a similar design and feature local wildlife. The Lake District Pound (LD£) is a local currency established _____ this ____year in the Lake District. Like many hyperlocal currencies all over the world, it functions as legal tender only within the boundaries of the National Park itself.

Yet according to the kind woman who runs the cashier stand at the Windemere National Park Service gift shop, tourists will go to extra trouble just to ask for their change in the LD£.

The Lake District Pound's official motto is: "Let's Keep the Lake District Special."

Only time will tell if Brexit and the slow, steady decline of the GBP will give way to this bright new currency.
